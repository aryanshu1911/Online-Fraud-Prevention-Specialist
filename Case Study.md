💰 Case Study: Vizag Cryptocurrency Investment Scam
In late 2025, a couple from Visakhapatnam (Vizag), Andhra Pradesh, lost nearly ₹1.5 crore (~$180,000) after being lured into a fraudulent cryptocurrency investment scheme. The scam involved convincing the victims to invest in digital assets through what appeared to be a legitimate online trading platform. Police have since registered a case, underscoring the sophistication of cybercriminals targeting unsuspecting investors and the growing prevalence of crypto‑related scams in India. The Times of India

📍 What Happened — Timeline of Events
Initial Contact via Social Media / Link:
The fraud began when the victims were approached with a link to an online crypto trading platform, which the scammers presented as a legitimate way to invest in USDT (Tether). Scammers assisted the couple in creating login credentials for the platform.

Small Initial Investments and Withdrawals:
To build confidence, the platform initially allowed the couple to invest small amounts and then showed minor profits that could be withdrawn. These early successes lowered the victims’ suspicion and made the scheme appear authentic.

Large Transactions:
Upon gaining trust, the couple transferred ₹75 lakh and ₹69 lakh in separate transactions to various bank accounts linked to the platform, expecting substantial profits from their investments. 

Withdrawal Denied and “Security Fee” Demand:
When the couple attempted to withdraw larger amounts, the fraudulent platform refused their request and demanded an additional *30 % of their total balance as a “risk assessment security fee.” Unable to pay such a high fee, the victims were left stuck — and shortly after, the platform changed its domain and communication ceased.

Police Case Filed:
Cybercrime police registered a case of cheating and criminal breach of trust and began investigating the incident, highlighting how cybercriminals continue to evolve their tactics to lure victims into fake financial schemes.

🔎 Detailed Breakdown of What Made This Scam Work
1. Trust‑Building with Small Wins
The fraudsters initially allowed small investments and seemingly genuine withdrawals, giving victims the belief that the platform was legitimate. This tactic erodes typical investor scepticism and encourages larger commitments.

2. Fabricated Platform Appearance
The trading site likely displayed account balances and profit figures that appeared credible, creating the illusion of successful trading activity. This visual reinforcement is a key manipulation tactic in investment scams.

3. Gradual Escalation of Financial Commitment
Once initial small investments were reinforced with superficial success, the victims were persuaded to transfer far larger sums — a common psychological escalation pattern used by fraudsters. 

4. Withdrawal Block and Landing Fee Demand
When the victims attempted to cash out larger sums, they were told a “risk assessment security fee” was required, effectively trapping the funds and prompting more emotional investment. The platform then changed domain addresses and went dark once the scammers had extracted as much money as possible. 

📌 Why Law Enforcement Treats It as Investment/Financial Fraud
Authorities have characterised this case as a cyber investment fraud rather than simple theft because:

The scam involved a fake trading platform designed to mimic legitimate crypto investment services. 

Fraudsters used professional‑looking account dashboards and profit figures to reduce scepticism. 

Initial withdrawals were allowed to build victim confidence. 

The cycle of investment → small payout → larger investment → denied withdrawal → additional fee demand reflects known investment scam patterns. 

These features align with how police classify and investigate cheating, breach of trust, and financial fraud cases tied to online investment schemes. 

📍 Unique Elements of This Case
Unlike some crypto scams that ask for upfront payment, this scam used a staged credibility progression:

First: small profits to build trust

Then: larger investments

Finally: locked withdrawals and fee demands before disappearing

This progression shows how fraudsters exploit confirmation bias — victims begin to trust and rationalise early outcomes, even when underlying signals are weak or suspicious. 

🐾 Aftermath and Public Response
Police Action: Cybercrime authorities have registered FIRs and begun investigations into the fraudulent scheme.

Public Warnings: Officials have urged the public not to trust unsolicited investment opportunities on social media. 

Consumer Advice: Authorities recommend only investing through verified channels, regulated platforms, and after proper due diligence. 

Reporting Guidance: Victims and the public are encouraged to report cybercrime via helplines and through local police units. 

🎯 Key Learning Points From This Specific Case
Fake platforms can appear credible with initial small returns and professional dashboards, making them especially deceptive.

Gradual escalation of financial commitment is a common strategy used by investment scammers to maximise victim loss. 

Requests for unlocking or “security fee” payments after initial investment are almost always red flags.

Changing domain addresses and sudden disappearance are signs of fraudulent platforms closing up once funds are extracted.

Law enforcement classification and reporting help place this in the broader context of online financial fraud, emphasising investigation and prevention.
